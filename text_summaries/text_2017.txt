<p>The 2017 General Election resulted in a hung Parliament, with no party winning an overall majority. The Conservative Party won the largest number of seats and votes, taking 317 seats and 42.3% of the vote,&nbsp;up from 36.8% in 2015. The Labour Party won 262 seats, and 40.0% of the vote, up from 232 seats and 30.4% of the vote in 2015. The Liberal Democrats won 12 seats, a net gain of 4 seats, and 7.4% of the vote.</p>
<p>The Scottish National Party won 35 seats, down from 56 seats in 2015. Plaid Cymru won 4 seats in Wales, one more than in 2015. In Northern Ireland, the Democratic Unionist Party (DUP) won 10 seats, gaining two, while Sinn F&eacute;in won 7, gaining 3.</p>

<p>70 seats changed hands.The Conservatives made 20 gains, but lost 33 seats for a net change of -13. Labour gained 36 seats and lost six, for a net change of +30. The Liberal Democrats lost four seats but gained eight, for a net change of +4. The SNP lost 21 seats and made no gains.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" loading="lazy" src="http://researchbriefings.files.parliament.uk/documents/CBP-7979/assets/67cc8d61-65cd-4996-a765-ec5a17633b74.png" alt="" width="460" height="230"/></p>
<p>Between them, the Conservatives and Labour won 82.3% of the UK vote &#8211; the highest since 1970. This is a notable increase compared with 2015, when their combined vote share was 67.2%. The Conservative vote share was highest in the East of England (54.6%), whereas the&nbsp;Labour vote share was highest in the North East (55.4%). UKIP&rsquo;s share of the vote fell from 12.6% in 2015 to 1.8% in 2017. The Green Party saw its share halved to 1.8%.</p>
<p>Turnout was 68.8%, up from 66.3% in 2015, the highest General Election turnout since 1997. The total registered electorate was 46.8 million, up from 46.4 million in 2015.</p>
<p>The South West had the highest turnout (71.8%), whereas Northern Ireland had the lowest (65.4%). Turnout increased across&nbsp;all regions and nations in the UK, apart from Scotland where it fell by 4.6% points.</p>
